Module 2: The Power of Universal Gates - NAND & NOR

July 2, 2026 • 8 min read

While the AND, OR, and NOT gates represent the conceptual foundations of Boolean logic, physical hardware design frequently relies on an entirely different standard: the Universal Gates. The NAND (NOT-AND) and NOR (NOT-OR) gates are elevated to the status of "universal" because any conceivable Boolean function, no matter how profoundly complex, can be physically implemented using only one type of these gates.

This principle of functional completeness is massively advantageous in industrial semiconductor manufacturing. Fabricating a silicon wafer comprising millions of identical NAND gates is vastly more economical and yields a higher structural density than fabricating a heterogeneous mixture of distinct AND, OR, and NOT gates.

The NAND and NOR Gates

The NAND gate is a compound logic gate that represents an AND gate followed sequentially by a NOT gate. Its output is '0' if and only if all inputs are '1'; in all other permutations, its output is '1'. The Boolean expression is Y=(A⋅B)'.

Conversely, the NOR gate represents an OR gate followed by an inverter. Its output is '1' if and only if all inputs are strictly '0'; otherwise, its output is '0'. The mathematical expression is Y=(A+B)'.

Building a NOT Gate from a NAND Gate

To demonstrate the staggering power of universal gates, one can mathematically prove how a simple NAND gate can be reconfigured to emulate every basic logical operation. To create a NOT gate from a NAND gate, an engineer simply ties all input terminals of the NAND gate together.

If the unified input A is '0', the NAND evaluates (0⋅0)' = 0' = 1. If the input is '1', it evaluates (1⋅1)' = 1' = 0. Thus, a single NAND gate acts as a perfect inverter.

Synthesizing AND and OR using NAND

Synthesizing an AND gate using NAND logic requires two sequential NAND gates. The primary inputs A and B are fed into the first NAND gate, which produces the intermediate signal (A⋅B)'. This intermediate signal is then routed into a second NAND gate configured as an inverter. The double inversion cancels out, yielding A⋅B.

Synthesizing an OR gate from NAND logic is slightly more complex and relies heavily on DeMorgan's Theorems. DeMorgan's Theorems are absolute pillars of digital logic, stating that the complement of a logical product is equal to the logical sum of the complements, and the complement of a logical sum is equal to the logical product of the complements.

To build an OR gate utilizing NANDs, the primary inputs A and B are first individually inverted using two NAND gates configured as NOT gates, producing A' and B'. These inverted signals are then fed into a third NAND gate. The final expression evaluates as (A'⋅B')'. By applying DeMorgan's Theorem, the double negations are broken, resulting in A+B, successfully achieving the OR operation utilizing exclusively NAND architecture.
