Module 7: Sequential Logic - The D-Flip Flop

July 7, 2026 • 10 min read

Everything discussed up to this point has been combinational logic: circuits with no memory, where the output is determined instantaneously by the inputs. To build a functioning computer, a system must be able to remember its previous state. This introduces the dimension of time, achieved through Sequential Logic.

The Master Clock

Sequential systems are globally synchronized by a master clock signal. The clock is a continuous, precisely regulated square wave oscillating between logic '0' and logic '1'. In modern microprocessors, this oscillator cycles billions of times per second (gigahertz). The transitions of the clock—specifically the rising edge (when the signal jumps from 0 to 1) or the falling edge (from 1 to 0)—dictate the exact moment when the entire system is allowed to update its memory states. This strict regimentation prevents chaotic data corruption during propagation delays.

The D-Flip Flop (Data Flip Flop)

The atomic unit of sequential memory is the Flip-Flop, constructed by routing the outputs of logic gates back into their own inputs, creating a bistable feedback loop. The most ubiquitous and important variant is the Data Flip-Flop, or D-Flip Flop.

The D-Flip Flop has two primary inputs: the Data input (D) and the Clock input (CLK). It has two outputs: Q (the stored memory bit) and Q' (the inverse of Q).

The operational rule of the D-Flip Flop is remarkably simple but profoundly powerful: On the active edge of the clock signal, whatever logical state is present at the D input is captured and transferred to the Q output. Between clock edges, the D input is completely ignored, and the Q output remains locked at its previously stored value, indefinitely holding that bit of memory.

By arraying 32 or 64 D-Flip Flops in parallel, engineers create the memory registers that hold the operands and instructions actively being processed by the CPU.
