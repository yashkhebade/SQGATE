Architecting an 8-Bit ALU

By SQGATE Engineering | Published on July 2, 2026 | 12 min read

Introduction to the Arithmetic Logic Unit (ALU)

The Arithmetic Logic Unit, universally known as the ALU, is the pulsing mathematical heart of any Central Processing Unit (CPU). It is the computational engine responsible for carrying out integer arithmetic and bitwise logic operations. Without the ALU, a microprocessor would be relegated to merely moving data around without the capability to manipulate, evaluate, or transform it. Historically, the architectural elegance of 8-bit processors like the MOS Technology 6502 and the Zilog Z80 relied heavily on the efficient design of their ALUs. Today, studying the intricacies of an 8-bit ALU provides profound insights into modern digital logic design and computer architecture.

In this technical deep dive, we will peel back the layers of abstraction and design an 8-bit ALU entirely from scratch using fundamental logic gates: AND, OR, XOR, NOT, and multiplexers. Our journey will traverse the foundational theory of binary arithmetic, the nuances of Two's Complement subtraction, the simultaneous execution of bitwise operations, the multiplexing logic required for instruction decoding, and the critical flag generation mechanisms that give the processor its conditional branching capabilities. Let’s embark on architecting this vital silicon component.

The Data Path and Bus Architecture

Before diving into the logic gates, we must establish the interface of our 8-bit ALU. The ALU operates on two primary input data buses, traditionally labeled A and B. Since we are architecting an 8-bit ALU, these buses are exactly 8 wires wide, capable of representing values from 0 to 255 in unsigned binary, or -128 to 127 in Two's Complement signed binary.

Alongside the data inputs, the ALU requires an operation code (opcode) input. For our basic design, a 3-bit control signal (let's call it ALU_OP) will suffice, granting us the ability to encode up to 8 distinct operations. Finally, there is the Carry-In (Cin) signal, vital for chaining ALUs together or for specific arithmetic instructions like 'Add with Carry' (ADC) or subtraction.

The output interface is equally important. The primary output is an 8-bit data bus representing the computed Result (Y). But the ALU's job is not complete once the calculation is done. It must also report the status of the operation via the Flags Register. These flags typically include Zero (Z), Negative (N), Carry-Out (C), and Overflow (V). The seamless interaction between these input and output signals forms the backbone of the ALU data path.

The Arithmetic Core: Building the Full Adder

The most complex and resource-intensive operation within our basic ALU is addition. Binary addition mimics the column-by-column method we use in base-10 arithmetic, including the concept of carrying over to the next column. The fundamental building block for this process is the Full Adder.

A Full Adder takes three 1-bit inputs: A, B, and Cin (Carry-In), and produces two 1-bit outputs: Sum and Cout (Carry-Out). The Boolean logic equations governing the Full Adder are beautifully symmetric:

Sum  = A ^ B ^ Cin
Cout = (A & B) | (Cin & (A ^ B))

To scale this to an 8-bit architecture, we cascade eight Full Adders together in a configuration known as a Ripple Carry Adder (RCA). The Cout of bit 0 is physically wired to the Cin of bit 1, the Cout of bit 1 feeds bit 2, and so forth, rippling all the way to bit 7. While mathematically sound, the RCA suffers from a significant propagation delay. The final sum for the most significant bit (MSB) cannot be finalized until the carry signal has rippled through all preceding seven stages. This creates the longest path of sequential logic delays, often referred to as the Critical Path.

In high-performance ALUs, this latency is unacceptable. Engineers circumvent this physical limitation by employing a Carry Lookahead Adder (CLA). The CLA operates on the principles of Carry Generate (G) and Carry Propagate (P). By evaluating these states simultaneously across multiple bits, the CLA calculates the carry for higher-order bits in parallel, drastically reducing the critical path delay. However, for a foundational 8-bit educational architecture, the Ripple Carry Adder offers a perfect balance of simplicity and functionality.

Mastering Subtraction with Two's Complement

Implementing subtraction in hardware could theoretically require a completely separate 'Subtractor' circuit. However, digital logic design favors efficiency and reuse. We can mathematically convert a subtraction problem into an addition problem using the magic of Two's Complement arithmetic. The fundamental mathematical identity we rely on is:

A - B = A + (-B)

In the Two's Complement system, the negative representation of a binary number is obtained by inverting all its bits (One's Complement) and adding 1. Thus, the equation becomes:

A - B = A + NOT(B) + 1

This mathematical transformation allows us to reuse our existing 8-bit adder block. The hardware implementation is remarkably elegant. We place an XOR gate on each of the 8 bits of the B input before it reaches the adder. The other leg of each XOR gate is tied to a control signal called SUB. When SUB = 0 (Addition), the B input passes through the XOR gates unchanged. When SUB = 1 (Subtraction), the XOR gates act as controlled inverters, flipping every bit of B. Crucially, the SUB signal is also tied directly to the Cin of the very first Full Adder (bit 0). This brilliantly accomplishes the "+ 1" requirement of Two's Complement, completely transforming the Adder into a Subtractor with minimal hardware overhead.

Simultaneous Bitwise Logic Operations

An ALU must perform more than just arithmetic; it must also execute bitwise logic operations such as AND, OR, and XOR. These operations are essential for masking bits, setting flags, and cryptographic algorithms. Unlike addition, bitwise operations are strictly independent for each bit—there is no carrying or borrowing between adjacent bits. This means bitwise operations are inherently parallel and extremely fast.

Within our ALU architecture, we instantiate an array of logic gates connected directly to the A and B buses. For an 8-bit ALU, this means we have 8 AND gates, 8 OR gates, and 8 XOR gates continuously evaluating the inputs. Whenever data is present on the A and B buses, the arithmetic core is calculating the sum, the AND array is computing the logical AND, the OR array is computing the logical OR, and the XOR array is computing the logical XOR—all simultaneously.

This parallel evaluation is a hallmark of combinational logic. The hardware does not wait to "decide" which operation to perform before calculating it; it calculates everything at once, constantly drawing power to evaluate all possible results. The responsibility of selecting the correct result falls to the multiplexer.

Instruction Decoding and The ALU Multiplexer

Since the ALU continuously generates the results for Addition/Subtraction, AND, OR, and XOR simultaneously, we need a mechanism to select which of these calculated results is routed to the final output bus (Y). This is the role of the Multiplexer (MUX).

We deploy an 8-bit wide, 4-to-1 Multiplexer. The selection pins of this MUX are driven by the ALU Opcode from the CPU's instruction decoder. Let's define a simple 2-bit opcode field for operation selection:

  • Opcode 00: Select the output of the Adder/Subtractor block.
  • Opcode 01: Select the output of the Bitwise AND array.
  • Opcode 10: Select the output of the Bitwise OR array.
  • Opcode 11: Select the output of the Bitwise XOR array.

This multiplexer acts as the final traffic controller, funneling the dynamically chosen 8-bit result onto the output bus while discarding the results of the unselected operations. This architecture ensures a constant, deterministic propagation delay for all ALU instructions, simplifying the overall CPU timing constraints.

Designing the Flags Register (Z, N, C, V)

The result on the output bus is only half the story. The CPU relies on the ALU to report the condition of the result to make branching decisions (like "Jump if Zero" or "Branch if Minus"). This is achieved via the Status Flags.

The Zero Flag (Z)

The Zero flag asserts high (1) only when all 8 bits of the final output (Y) are exactly zero. In hardware, this is implemented using a massive 8-input NOR gate tied to the output bus. Z = ~(Y0 | Y1 | Y2 | Y3 | Y4 | Y5 | Y6 | Y7). If even a single bit is a 1, the NOR gate outputs a 0.

The Negative Flag (N)

In Two's Complement representation, the Most Significant Bit (MSB)—which is bit 7 in an 8-bit system—always indicates the sign. If bit 7 is 1, the number is negative. If bit 7 is 0, the number is positive. Therefore, the Negative flag is simply a direct wire connection to the 7th bit of the output bus. N = Y7.

The Carry Flag (C)

The Carry flag is primarily used for unsigned arithmetic. It is directly tied to the Cout of the final (8th) Full Adder. If an addition results in a value greater than 255, the Carry flag is set. It is also utilized in multi-byte arithmetic (like adding 16-bit numbers on an 8-bit CPU) where the Carry from the lower byte becomes the Carry-In for the upper byte.

The Overflow Flag (V)

The Overflow flag is arguably the most complex, as it specifically targets signed Two's Complement arithmetic. It indicates when an arithmetic operation has exceeded the signed capacity of the 8-bit bus (-128 to +127). For example, adding 100 + 100 equals 200, which exceeds 127 and wraps around to a negative number in Two's Complement. The hardware logic for detecting overflow relies on analyzing the carry-in to the MSB and the carry-out from the MSB. The Boolean equation is: V = Cin7 ^ Cout7. Alternatively, it can be calculated by comparing the signs of the inputs and the output: V = (~A7 & ~B7 & Sum7) | (A7 & B7 & ~Sum7). If two positive numbers yield a negative sum, or two negative numbers yield a positive sum, an overflow has definitively occurred.

Conclusion

Architecting an 8-bit ALU from scratch is a rite of passage for computer engineers. It strips away the mystery of how computers "think" and reveals a beautiful, deterministic cascade of logic gates. By understanding how the Full Adders ripple, how Two's complement effortlessly handles subtraction, how multiplexers route data, and how flags monitor the state of the machine, you gain an intimate appreciation for the foundations of computer science. This 8-bit core, while seemingly rudimentary compared to modern 64-bit superscalar architectures, operates on the exact same fundamental principles of Boolean algebra that power today's supercomputers.
