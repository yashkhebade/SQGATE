Sequential Logic: Master the D-Flip Flop

June 30, 2026 • 7 min read

Combinational logic (like the adders we saw earlier) has no memory. The output depends entirely on the current input. If you change the input, the output changes instantly.

But computers need memory! They need to store data, count numbers, and step through instructions. This is where Sequential Logic and Flip-Flops come into play.

The D-Flip Flop and the Clock

A D-Flip Flop (Data Flip Flop) stores exactly 1 bit of data. However, it only looks at its input D when it is told to by a Clock signal.

The Clock is a continuous oscillating signal (0, 1, 0, 1...). Most D-Flip Flops are Edge-Triggered. This means they only capture the value of D exactly at the moment the Clock transitions from 0 to 1 (the rising edge). At all other times, they ignore D and hold their stored value at output Q.

Building Registers

By wiring 8 D-Flip Flops together so they share the same Clock signal, you create an 8-bit Register! This is the exact mechanism CPUs use to store variables and pointers.
