Metastability and Clock Domain Crossing (CDC) in Digital Design

By Yash Khebade | Published on July 01, 2026 | 1 min read

Introduction to Metastability

In digital electronics, flip-flops are designed to output either a distinct logic HIGH (1) or a LOW (0). However, if the input data changes precisely at the moment the clock edge triggers (violating the setup or hold time), the flip-flop can enter an unstable, intermediate state known as metastability.

During metastability, the output voltage hovers unpredictably between 0 and 1 before eventually settling to a valid state. This unpredictable delay can cause downstream logic to read incorrect or conflicting values, leading to catastrophic system failure.

Clock Domain Crossing (CDC)

Metastability typically occurs when a signal travels from one clock domain to another asynchronous clock domain. Because the two clocks have different frequencies or phase alignments, it's impossible to guarantee that the signal will not violate the destination flip-flop's setup or hold time.

The Two-Flop Synchronizer

The standard solution to mitigate metastability is the Two-Flop Synchronizer. By chaining two flip-flops back-to-back in the destination clock domain, we give the metastable signal an entire clock cycle to settle before it is sampled by the rest of the circuit.

While this introduces a delay (latency of two clock cycles), it exponentially increases the Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF) from mere milliseconds to billions of years, making the circuit reliable.
