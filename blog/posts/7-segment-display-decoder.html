Designing a BCD to 7-Segment Display Decoder

Published: July 6, 2026  •  8 min read

If you've ever looked at a digital clock, a calculator, or a retro electronic scoreboard, you've seen a 7-segment display. These iconic components use seven individual LED segments (labeled a through g) to form the numbers 0 through 9.

But how does a computer actually tell the display which segments to light up? The secret lies in a combinational logic circuit known as a BCD (Binary-Coded Decimal) to 7-Segment Decoder.

Core Concept: A decoder takes a 4-bit binary input (representing numbers 0-9) and outputs 7 unique signals to turn the appropriate segments ON or OFF.

Step 1: Understanding the BCD Input

Our circuit will take four input variables: A, B, C, and D. Since 4 bits can represent 16 unique values (0000 to 1111), but we only care about the decimal digits 0 through 9 (0000 to 1001), the remaining six combinations (1010 to 1111) are considered "Don't Cares."

Step 2: Defining the Truth Table

To design our logic gates, we first need to map out exactly what each of the seven outputs (a, b, c, d, e, f, g) should be doing for every input combination.

For example, to display the number "0" (Input: 0000), we need segments a, b, c, d, e, and f to be ON (1), and segment g (the middle bar) to be OFF (0).

Step 3: Minimizing Logic with Karnaugh Maps

Once we have the Truth Table, we can extract the Boolean expressions for each segment. However, the raw Sum-of-Products (SOP) expression will be massive and require too many gates to build efficiently.

To reduce the number of logic gates, we use Karnaugh Maps (K-Maps). By grouping the 1s (and strategically grouping our "Don't Cares"), we can drastically simplify the Boolean algebra.

For instance, the simplified expression for segment a becomes:
a = A + C + (B & D) + (~B & ~D)

Step 4: Building the Circuit in SQGATE

With the minimized Boolean equations in hand, it is simply a matter of wiring up the logic gates! You will need a mix of AND, OR, and NOT gates.

Modern decoders, like the classic 74LS47 integrated circuit, often have active-low outputs, meaning you actually send a '0' to light up a segment. However, when building this in the SQGATE Simulator, you can use active-high logic and connect your outputs directly to the 7-segment display component provided in the output tools menu.
